The Principles of Computing

4000 BC – 1890
4000 BC

The Abacus

The first known calculating device, the abacus, was used in ancient Mesopotamia around 4000 BC. Used for basic arithmetic, the abacus used a system of sliding beads on rows to represent numbers, enabling merchants and administrators to perform calculations quickly and accurately — establishing the foundational concept of representing numbers with physical objects.

CALCULATING DEVICE
1614

Logarithms — John Napier

Scottish mathematician John Napier published his discovery of logarithms — a mathematical concept that transforms multiplication into addition, dramatically simplifying complex calculations. Napier also invented Napier's Bones, a set of numbered rods that allowed users to perform multiplication and division mechanically, directly inspiring future calculating machines.

MATHEMATICS
1642

Pascaline — First Mechanical Calculator

French mathematician Blaise Pascal invented the Pascaline, the first practical mechanical calculator, to help his father with tax calculations. The device used a system of interlocking gears to perform addition and subtraction — representing the first time mechanical automation was applied to arithmetic computation.

MECHANICAL CALCULATOR
1673

Step Reckoner — Gottfried Leibniz

German philosopher Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz created the Step Reckoner, which could perform all four basic arithmetic operations. More importantly, Leibniz developed the binary number system — the foundation of all modern digital computing — proposing that all numbers could be represented using only 0s and 1s.

BINARY SYSTEM
1801

Jacquard Loom — Punched Card Control

French weaver Joseph Marie Jacquard invented a loom controlled by punched cards to automate the weaving of complex patterns. This was the first use of punched cards as a programming mechanism — a concept that would directly inspire Charles Babbage and later become the dominant input method for computers well into the 1970s.

PUNCHED CARDS
1822

Difference Engine — Charles Babbage

English mathematician Charles Babbage designed the Difference Engine, a mechanical calculator designed to automatically produce mathematical tables by calculating polynomial equations. Though never fully completed in his lifetime, it established the concept of an automatic computing machine and inspired all subsequent computer designs.

MECHANICAL COMPUTER
1843

Ada Lovelace — First Algorithm

Ada Lovelace, working with Babbage's Analytical Engine, wrote what is considered the first computer algorithm — a step-by-step set of instructions designed to be processed by a machine. She also conceptualized that computing machines could go beyond pure calculation, anticipating software and general-purpose computing over a century before it became reality.

FIRST PROGRAMMER
1847

Boolean Algebra — George Boole

English mathematician George Boole published "The Mathematical Analysis of Logic," creating Boolean algebra — a system where logical statements are expressed as equations with values of TRUE or FALSE (1 or 0). This became the mathematical foundation of all digital circuit design and computer logic over a century later.

LOGIC & MATHEMATICS
1890

Hollerith Tabulating Machine

American inventor Herman Hollerith developed an electromechanical tabulating machine that used punched cards to process the 1890 U.S. Census — reducing the count from 8 years to just 18 months. Hollerith later founded a company that would become IBM, directly linking this invention to the origins of the modern computing industry.

The Open Hardware Boom

2000s
2003

PowerMac G5 — First 64-bit Personal Computer

Hailed as the first true 64-bit personal computer, the Apple G5 was the most powerful Macintosh released to that point. Virginia Tech used more than a thousand PowerMac G5s to create the System X cluster supercomputer, ranked #3 fastest in the world in November 2003.

64-BIT COMPUTING
2005

Arduino — The Maker Movement

Beginning as a project of the Interaction Design Institute in Ivrea, Italy, each credit card-sized Arduino board offered an inexpensive microcontroller ideal for connecting to the outside world. Arduino soon became the main platform of the worldwide "Maker" movement, inspiring millions of hobbyists and students.

OPEN-SOURCE HARDWARE
2008

MacBook Air — The Ultra-Notebook

Apple introduced the first ultra-thin notebook with a high-capacity battery and integrated camera. To reduce its size, the MacBook Air replaced the traditional hard drive with a solid-state disk — making it the first mass-market computer to do so and setting the standard for all modern thin laptops.

The Early Digital Era

1950s
1951

UNIVAC I — First Commercial Computer

The UNIVAC I demonstrated the commercial viability of electronic digital computing for business and government data processing, marking a transition from experimental systems to production machines.

COMMERCIAL COMPUTING
1951

Whirlwind / Magnetic Core Memory

The Whirlwind project at MIT led to the development of magnetic core memory, a reliable high-speed RAM technology that became dominant for decades.

MEMORY TECHNOLOGY
1952

Grace Hopper — A-0 and Compiler Work

Grace Hopper developed early tools (A-0) that paved the way for modern compilers and higher-level programming, helping make software development more accessible.

PROGRAMMING
1954

IBM 650 — Mass Adoption

The IBM 650 became one of the first mass-produced computers, widely adopted by universities and businesses and serving as a training ground for early programmers.

MASS ADOPTION
1956

RAMAC — First Disk Drive

IBM's RAMAC introduced the first magnetic disk storage system, allowing random access to large amounts of data and transforming I/O capabilities for computing systems.

STORAGE
1957

FORTRAN — High-Level Programming

IBM released FORTRAN, one of the first widely used high-level languages, which greatly simplified scientific programming and established compiler technology as a core field.

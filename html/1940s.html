The Electronic Dawn

1940s
1943

Neural Networks — McCulloch & Pitts

Scientists Warren S. McCulloch and Walter H. Pitts published "A Logical Calculus of the Ideas Immanent in Nervous Activity," a foundational work in the study of artificial neural networks and artificial intelligence research.

NEURAL NETWORKS
1944

Colossus at Bletchley Park

Designed by British engineer Tommy Flowers, the Colossus used up to 2,500 vacuum tubes to break complex Nazi Lorenz ciphers. It reduced decryption time from weeks to hours and significantly shortened World War II. Its existence was kept secret until the 1970s.

CODEBREAKING
1944

Harvard Mark I

Conceived by Harvard professor Howard Aiken and built by IBM, the Harvard Mark I was a room-sized relay-based calculator with a fifty-foot camshaft synchronizing thousands of parts and using 3,500 relays. It produced mathematical tables but was soon superseded by electronic computers.

RELAY COMPUTER
1945

Von Neumann Architecture

Mathematician John von Neumann outlined the architecture of a stored-program computer in a widely circulated paper, including electronic storage of programming information and data — eliminating the need for plugboards and punched cards. This architecture remains the basis of modern computers.

COMPUTER ARCHITECTURE
1946

ENIAC — Public Unveiling

Built by John Mauchly and J. Presper Eckert at the University of Pennsylvania, ENIAC was over 1,000 times faster than any previous computer. It occupied more than 1,000 square feet, used about 18,000 vacuum tubes, and weighed 30 tons.

ELECTRONIC COMPUTER
1947

First Computer Bug — Grace Hopper

On September 9th, Grace Hopper recorded the first actual computer bug — a moth found inside a relay of the Harvard Mark II computer. Hopper later developed what is considered the first compiler (A-0) and helped create COBOL, a programming language still in use today.

PROGRAMMING HISTORY
1948

The Bit — Claude Shannon

American mathematician Claude Shannon wrote "The Mathematical Theory of Communication," identifying the bit as the fundamental unit of information and the basic unit of computation — laying the groundwork for all modern digital communications.

INFORMATION THEORY
1948

Manchester "Baby" — First Stored Program Run

Researchers at the University of Manchester ran the first program in history on a digital, electronic, stored-program computer on June 21st, 1948. The 17-instruction program, written by Tom Kilburn, marked a watershed moment in the history of computing.

STORED-PROGRAM COMPUTER
1949

EDSAC — First Practical Stored-Program Computer

Built at Cambridge University under Professor Maurice Wilkes, EDSAC was the first practical stored-program computer to provide a regular computing service. Wilkes pioneered "subroutines" — small reusable programs stored on punched paper tape — a concept fundamental to modern software.

STORED-PROGRAM
1949

Birth of the Modem

Developed to transmit radar signals, the modem converted digital data into sounds and demodulated received sounds back into digital data. Commercialized by Bell Telephone in 1958, modems allowed computers to communicate over ordinary voice telephone lines worldwide.

TELECOMMUNICATIONS
1949

MIT Magnetic Core Memory

At MIT, Jay Forrester described his "coincident current" technique for magnetic core memory — the first reliable high-speed random access memory for computers. This technology remained dominant well into the 1970s.

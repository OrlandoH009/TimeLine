The Electronic Dawn

1940s
1940

Complex Number Calculator (CNC)

George Stibitz and colleagues completed the Complex Number Calculator (CNC) at Bell Labs, demonstrating remote access computation via teleprinter — an early example of interacting with a computer from a distance.

REMOTE COMPUTING
1941

Z3 — Konrad Zuse

The Z3, built by Konrad Zuse, was a fully functional electromechanical programmable computer using relays and floating-point arithmetic, and is considered one of the first working program-controlled machines.

PROGRAMMABLE COMPUTER
1942

Atanasoff–Berry Computer (ABC)

John Atanasoff and Clifford Berry developed the ABC, an electronic digital computing prototype that introduced important ideas in binary arithmetic and electronic computation.

ELECTRONIC COMPUTING
1944

Colossus — Bletchley Park

The Colossus machines, designed by Tommy Flowers, used vacuum tubes to perform high-speed processing for codebreaking of Lorenz ciphers, significantly aiding Allied intelligence efforts during World War II.

CRYPTOANALYSIS
1945

Von Neumann Report — EDVAC Architecture

John von Neumann outlined the stored-program computer architecture, formalizing the concept of storing instructions and data in electronic memory — the foundation of modern computers.

ARCHITECTURE
1948

Manchester "Baby" — First Stored-Program Run

Researchers at the University of Manchester ran the first program on a stored-program electronic computer on June 21, 1948. The experiment demonstrated practical execution of programs stored in memory, a critical milestone for modern computing.

STORED-PROGRAM
1949

EDSAC — First Practical Stored-Program Service

Built at Cambridge University, EDSAC provided a practical stored-program service and introduced reusable subroutines, making electronic computing more accessible to researchers.

The Mechanical Era

1930s
1931

Differential Analyzer — Vannevar Bush

MIT engineer Vannevar Bush built the first large-scale automatic general-purpose mechanical analog computer, the Differential Analyzer. Used to solve complex differential equations for artillery trajectories, the machine used a system of rotating shafts, gears, and wheels to perform integration — a mechanical precursor to the electronic computers that followed.

ANALOG COMPUTER
1936

Turing Machine — Alan Turing

British mathematician Alan Turing published "On Computable Numbers," introducing the concept of a theoretical computing device — now known as a Turing Machine. This abstract model described a device that could simulate any algorithmic process, establishing the mathematical foundations of computation, algorithm theory, and what would become computer science.

THEORETICAL COMPUTING
1938

Z1 — Konrad Zuse

German engineer Konrad Zuse built the Z1, a mechanical binary programmable computer in his parents' living room in Berlin. The Z1 was the first computer to use binary floating-point numbers and Boolean logic — and the first machine to receive its instructions from a punched tape rather than manual settings. Zuse later built the relay-based Z3 in 1941, the first fully functional programmable computer.
